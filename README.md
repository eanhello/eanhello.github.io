<p style="padding-left: 30px">A r t i c l e s</p>
"In War Time: Whittier's Civil War Address and The Quaker Periodical Press." <i>Quaker Studies</i> 19.2 (2015): 229-242. Print.
<p style="padding-left: 30px">Find and Download the Article in CORE, <a href="https://mla.hcommons.org/deposits/item/mla:425/">HERE</a></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px"><strong>Abstract</strong>: Fought for a cause in which many Quakers ostensibly believed and had previously risked a great deal for, the American Civil War was a time of great trial for American Quakers. Extending ongoing efforts to understand this complex period, the following essay considers John G. Whittier’s mid-war poem, "In War Time," and the peculiarity of its simultaneous appearance in the three, divided Quaker periodicals of the nineteenth century. Specifically, this article argues that Whittier’s poem renders more legible the dilemma Friends faced when attempting to position their peace principles in ethical relation to the mass suffering of the war. Reflecting, while also contributing to, a major transformation in American Quakerism, "In War Time" questioned the ethics of dissociative pacifism, exhorting Quakers to assist the Union war effort, if only in non- combatant roles, as nurses in hospitals and as teachers among the freedmen.</p>

		<content:encoded><![CDATA[My hope is that when my students leave my classroom at the end of a quarter or semester, they do so with a commitment to using language self-consciously, recognizing that clarity and concision can be exercises in generosity and self-discipline. On the one hand, I challenge my students to appreciate the ways a strong, structured foundation in research and writing—along with and the creative tools of interpretation and collaboration offered in English Departments—are valuable and marketable skills in today’s economy. On the other, I believe that the study of literature enkindles the kind of critical thinking, compassion, and committed action that nourish a just society. Lifelong engagement with literature, I try to model, offers insight into the ways writing and language have influenced the values and lived experience of people across time and place.

C o u r s e s

<hr />
<p style="padding-left: 30px">Literary Portraiture: Writing People, Writing Places.</p>
I designed and led this iteration of English 105 (expository writing) in the Fall of 2015. The course reading focused on literary portraits of people, both as a general category or genre of literature, as well as a specific form of writing practice. Our discussions explored the various ways writers (mostly in the twentieth century) have approached portraiture, identifying the shared methods, intentions and implied ethics around “writing” another person and the places they inhabit. I mobilized the insights and awarenesses that my students developed between the readings and discussions in their writing assignments, challenging them to recognize how portraits ask us to become more aware of how we see and think about other people, and ourselves. I talked throughout the course about the work of revision, encouraging my students to see drafting and editing as stages in a larger process of clear, generous writing. Peer editing, along with routine, individual conferences with me, were major components of this expository writing course. I encouraged my students seek critical feedback, will guiding them through the process of giving thoughtful, precise, and encouraging written-feedback to their classmates. In all of my work as an instructor and TA I have asked my students to countenance critical and editorial feedback from me and other students, doing so with a balance of humility and confidence.

<h3 style="padding-left: 30px">"Joseph Breintnall and the Red Link: Building Wikipedia Pages in Early American History"</h3>
<h3>Lost</h3>
Last week, Joseph Breintnall (d. 1746) had no Wikipedia page. Breintnall was mentioned here and there in a modest handful of extant Wikipedia pages before I started writing this, but he had no Main page of his own. He was, in Wikipedia’s language, a “<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Red_link">Red Link</a>.” A “Red Link” is a deliberately placed link to a Web-page that does not yet exist. Red links, then, are an act of encyclopedic hope, a sign that indicates a desire to push against the boundries of what this incredible resource contains. I decided to build Wikipedia pages for both Breintnall and one of his major collaborative-projects with Benjamin Franklin.

The formal creation and acceptance of an appropriate page by the Wiki community turns the disparate red links scattered across the Encyclopedia BLUE and automatically redirects those links to the page when / if it ever emerges. The creation of a central “Joseph Breintnall” main page changes the Red links to active Blue Links, connecting far-flung pages to the new Main Page, so that now entries for Franklin's <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junto_(club)">Junto</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_Company_of_Philadelphia">The Library Company of Philadelphia</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Bradford">Andrew Bradford</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_printing">Nature Printing</a>, are all linked together through Breintnall.
<h3>Found</h3>
<h2><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Breintnall">Joseph Breintnall</a>   &amp;   "<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Busy-Body">The Busy-Body</a>"</h2>
Most of us have cautioned our students about the dubious and equivocal nature of Wikipedia as a robust research source. But I have found the creation / expansion / and connecting of Wikipedia pages relevant to course readings and material to be a challenging and incredibly useful exercise for my students. Non-major students filling a distribution requirement and  who might be hesitant to say much in class, will often find value in researching some aspect of the course and incrementally contributing to public knowledge through sites like Wikipedia.

I decided then to re-appropriate the bibliographic and narrative work of a recent conference paper into the foundation of a Wikipedia entry. While by nature an ongoing and profoundly collaborative endeavor, I am using the occasion of this entry to quietly launch both Joseph Breintnall’s Wikipedia page and that of the Busy Body. As of Wednesday or Thursday, both pages have undergone initial review by the editorial community and are active spaces, although Search Engines have yet to privilege them in response to queries, so are not yet front page results.

		<title>&quot;Joseph Breintnall and the Red Link: Building Wikipedia Pages in Early American History&quot;</title>
		<link>http://eanhigh.mla.hcommons.org/2017/01/10/joseph-breintnall-and-the-red-link-building-wikipedia-pages-in-early-american-history/</link>

		<content:encoded><![CDATA[<h3>Lost</h3>
Last week, Joseph Breintnall (d. 1746) had no Wikipedia page. Breintnall was mentioned here and there in a modest handful of extant Wikipedia pages before I started writing this, but he had no Main page of his own. He was, in Wikipedia’s language, a “<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Red_link">Red Link</a>.” A “Red Link” is a deliberately placed link to a Web-page that does not yet exist. Red links, then, are an act of encyclopedic hope, a sign that indicates a desire to push against the boundries of what this incredible resource contains. I decided to build Wikipedia pages for both Breintnall and one of his major collaborative-projects with Benjamin Franklin.

The formal creation and acceptance of an appropriate page by the Wiki community turns the disparate red links scattered across the Encyclopedia BLUE and automatically redirects those links to the page when / if it ever emerges. The creation of a central “Joseph Breintnall” main page changes the Red links to active Blue Links, connecting far-flung pages to the new Main Page, so that now entries for Franklin's <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junto_(club)">Junto</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_Company_of_Philadelphia">The Library Company of Philadelphia</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Bradford">Andrew Bradford</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_printing">Nature Printing</a>, are all linked together through Breintnall.
<h3>Found</h3>
<h2><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Breintnall">Joseph Breintnall</a>   &amp;   "<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Busy-Body">The Busy-Body</a>"</h2>
Most of us have cautioned our students about the dubious and equivocal nature of Wikipedia as a robust research source. But I have found the creation / expansion / and connecting of Wikipedia pages relevant to course readings and material to be a challenging and incredibly useful exercise for my students. Non-major students filling a distribution requirement and  who might be hesitant to say much in class, will often find value in researching some aspect of the course and incrementally contributing to public knowledge through sites like Wikipedia.

I decided then to re-appropriate the bibliographic and narrative work of a recent conference paper into the foundation of a Wikipedia entry. While by nature an ongoing and profoundly collaborative endeavor, I am using the occasion of this entry to quietly launch both Joseph Breintnall’s Wikipedia page and that of the Busy Body. As of Wednesday or Thursday, both pages have undergone initial review by the editorial community and are active spaces, although Search Engines have yet to privilege them in response to queries, so are not yet front page results.
